Why do SMS API rates vary so much by country?

Each destination network charges a different termination cost to deliver a message to its subscribers, so per-country rate differences reflect real carrier costs, not arbitrary markup. A flat headline rate is usually misleading for that reason.

Carriers in the US and parts of Europe sit near the low end of the scale. Routes into Australia or Japan land near the top because those networks charge more to terminate traffic. Our floor sits at $0.004 on the cheapest destinations. Most traffic lands between $0.009 and $0.035, with $0.035 reserved for the expensive AU and JP routes rather than anything we advertise as a headline.

When you compare providers, ask about the fees that sit outside the per-message number. Common ones include monthly minimums, setup charges, inactive account fees, sender ID registration markups, and charges for delivery reports or failed attempts that never get credited back. A rate that looks cheap on paper doubles once those line items appear.

We credit failed messages automatically. Unused balance returns to the originating wallet on request. That auto-refund mechanic keeps the effective cost honest. You pay for delivered traffic, not for noise. Free test credits on signup let you check a route before you fund anything. REST and SMPP are both available, with examples in Python, PHP, Go and Node.

What's a realistic price floor and range for a cheap SMS API?

A realistic price floor sits at $0.004 per message, with typical rates from $0.009 to $0.035 across 149 countries.

The $0.035 ceiling covers expensive routes such as Australia and Japan. An honest provider surfaces that upper bound instead of headlining the floor alone. Buyers who only see the lowest number later find most destinations cost more.

Check every route you will use against the full price list. The range exists because termination fees vary by network, and a provider that refuses to show the high end is not giving you the data needed to budget.

Your effective rate is the volume-weighted average across the countries you actually hit, not the teaser figure. Price the mix, not the marketing number.

What hidden costs do other 'cheap' providers add on top?

Number rental fees, business or local registration fees, monthly minimums or platform fees layered on the per-message rate, and zero credit for failed sends are the usual extras that inflate a low headline.

Ask every provider these questions directly before you fund an account. Does the quoted rate include a dedicated number, or do you pay a separate monthly rental just to originate traffic? Some routes also demand business or local registration paperwork and pass that cost straight to you as a setup or recurring line item. Platform fees and monthly minimums sit on top of the per-message charge even when your volume is light, so the effective cost per delivered SMS climbs fast once those fixed charges hit the invoice.

Failed deliveries matter just as much. If a provider keeps the money when a message never reaches the handset, your real spend is higher than the rate card suggests. Automatic credit for undelivered messages changes the math: you only pay for what actually lands. SMSRoute automatically credits failed messages back to the balance and will refund unused funds to the originating wallet on request, so the quoted rate is closer to the true out-of-pocket figure.

Run the same checklist on every quote you receive. The difference between a genuinely low all-in cost and a headline that looks cheap until the extras appear is exactly these four categories.

What happens to your cost when a message fails to deliver?

Failed messages are automatically credited back to your balance, so you pay only for what actually delivers, and unused funds refund to the originating wallet on request.

That auto-credit is the difference between a quoted rate and the real effective cost. Many providers charge the full amount the moment you hit send, then leave undelivered traffic as a sunk expense. Here the reverse happens: if the handset never accepts the message, the credit returns without a support ticket. You still see the attempt in the logs, but the balance moves back so the failed unit never stays on the bill.

Check the outcome yourself in two calls. POST /sms/send returns a messageId; follow it with GET /sms/single/:messageId/status for the live DLR. The same status appears in the dashboard log. When you compare providers, ask the blunt question: do failed sends get credited automatically, or do you keep the money regardless of delivery? A low headline rate that never refunds failures quietly raises your true cost per successful message.

Unused balance itself stays liquid. Request a refund to the same crypto wallet that funded the account and the remaining balance leaves. Combine that with free test credits on signup and you can verify routes and the refund path before committing real volume.

How do you estimate real cost for a bulk send before committing?

Pull per-destination rates from the API first, run a validate call as pre-flight, then fire a small bulk send with free test credits so you see real delivery cost before you fund anything large.

A blended headline rate hides the spread. Query prices for every country and network in your list instead of assuming one number covers the whole batch. Once you have those figures, POST a validate request against the exact payload and sender setup you plan to use. That catches format problems, unsupported destinations, and suppression hits before any charge lands.

With free test credits issued on signup you can then call the bulk send endpoint against a short recipient sample drawn from the same routes. Watch the real-time delivery reports and the automatic credit-back on any fails. The effective cost that appears in the transaction log is the number you multiply by your full volume. Only after that sample clears do you move balance in and scale the same call.

The whole loop from email signup to first verified bulk result takes minutes. You leave with hard per-route economics, not a guess.

Frequently asked questions

Does a monthly minimum ever make sense?

It can, at very high fixed volume with dedicated support, but a low or variable-volume sender often ends up paying for capacity they never use. Pay-per-message pricing avoids that mismatch entirely. Our 24/7 Telegram and email support typically replies in under an hour, so high volume users get help fast without a minimum commitment.

Is bulk pricing different from single-message pricing?

Bulk sending via POST /sms/send/bulk uses the same published per-country rates as a single send. Check current numbers at GET /prices rather than assuming a separate bulk discount exists. Adaptive multi-route delivery with automatic failover per destination keeps bulk jobs as reliable as single messages.

Does paying with crypto lower the per-message cost?

Not automatically. BTC, ETH, USDT (TRC-20/ERC-20), XMR, LTC, SOL simply fund the account as a payment preference, not a rate discount. Per-message pricing is set by destination and stays the same no matter which coin paid the balance. Real-time delivery reports arrive via DLR webhooks and the dashboard log for every send once you are funded.

Can you export cost reports for a bulk campaign?

Yes. The endpoints GET /sms/bulk/reports and GET /sms-bulk/:batchid/export return a full record of what a bulk campaign actually cost. That lets you reconcile the total against published per-country rates. Exports list every message status so automatic credits for failures show up clearly, and you can pull the same detail from the dashboard log anytime.

Does wider country coverage mean uniform pricing across countries?

No. Covering 149 countries does not mean uniform pricing. Rates vary by destination, so check each country's specific rate rather than a blended average. Adaptive multi-route delivery with automatic failover per destination keeps messages moving across those varied routes.
